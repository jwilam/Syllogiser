📚 Syllogiser - Help & User Guide

👋 Welcome

Welcome to Syllogiser! This educational platform helps you learn and understand categorical logic through interactive diagrams. Whether you're studying basic propositions or complex syllogistic arguments, this tool provides visual representations to enhance your understanding.

Syllogiser is unique in that it supports both Venn Diagrams and Euler Diagrams, making it a versatile tool for all your logical diagramming needs.

📂 Data Loading

You can load datasets from CSV files to quickly navigate through multiple propositions or syllogisms.

🔄 How to Use Data Loading

  1. Click the "📁 Load data" button at the top of the page
  2. Select a CSV or TXT file from your computer
  3. The data will be loaded based on the current mode (Statement or Syllogism)
  4. Use the blue navigation bar to browse through loaded items
  5. You can always manually edit any loaded data

Statement Mode CSV Format:

  • Each line has 4 elements: Quantifier,Subject,Copula,Predicate
  • Blocks are separated by at least one blank line
  • 1 line = Premise only; 2 lines = Premise + Conclusion
  • Blocks with 3+ lines are ignored

📄 Statement Mode Example

All,dogs,are,animals Some,cats,are not,friendly All,birds,are,animals No,fish,are,mammals Some,pets,are,fish

Syllogism Mode CSV Format:

  • Each line has 4 elements: Quantifier,Subject,Copula,Predicate
  • Blocks are separated by at least one blank line
  • 1 line = Premise 1 only; 2 lines = P1 + P2; 3 lines = P1 + P2 + Conclusion
  • Blocks with 4+ lines are ignored

📄 Syllogism Mode Example

All,men,are,mortal All,Greeks,are,men All,Greeks,are,mortal No,reptiles,are,mammals All,snakes,are,reptiles Some,students,are,athletes All,athletes,are,healthy

⚠️ Important Parsing Rules:

  • Quantifiers and Copulas must match the terms defined in Settings
  • Matching is case-insensitive
  • Spaces within terms are ignored
  • "No" quantifier with "are" copula maps to E-type
  • Invalid blocks are silently skipped

⭕ Euler Diagram Support

Syllogiser is (probably) the only application that supports both Venn Diagrams and Euler Diagrams for syllogism visualization.

🔄 How to Create Euler Diagrams

Unlike Venn diagrams where all circles overlap in a fixed pattern, Euler diagrams allow you to resize and reposition circles to show actual set relationships:

  • Resize circles: Drag the edge of any circle to make it larger or smaller
  • Move circles: Drag from the center of a circle to reposition it
  • Show containment: Make one circle smaller and place it entirely inside another
  • Show exclusion: Move circles apart so they don't overlap
  • Show partial overlap: Position circles to overlap partially
Venn Diagram

All possible intersections are shown. Empty regions are shaded to indicate they have no members. Fixed circle sizes and positions.

Euler Diagram

Only actual relationships are shown. Circle size and position indicate set relationships directly. More intuitive for some users.

💡 Tip: Use Diagram 1 for Euler diagrams and Diagram 2 for Venn diagrams to compare both representations side by side!

📏 Resizable Panels

You can adjust the size of the left and right panels to give yourself more space for diagramming:

  • Drag the divider between the left panel (propositions) and right panel (diagrams) to resize
  • Make the diagram larger by dragging the divider to the left
  • Make the propositions panel larger by dragging the divider to the right
  • Works with both mouse and touch - drag with your finger on mobile devices

💡 Tip: For detailed diagram work, drag the divider far to the left to maximize your canvas space while still keeping the propositions visible.

🔄 Two Operating Modes

The platform offers two modes accessible via the toggle switch at the top:

  • Statement Mode: For analyzing single categorical propositions (A, E, I, O types) with two-circle diagrams
  • Syllogism Mode: For analyzing categorical syllogisms with three-circle diagrams, including validity checking and formal proving

💡 Tip: Start with Statement Mode to understand basic propositions before moving to Syllogism Mode.

📝 Categorical Proposition Types

There are four standard forms of categorical propositions:

TypeNameFormSubjectPredicate
AUniversal AffirmativeAll S are PDistributedUndistributed
EUniversal NegativeNo S are PDistributedDistributed
IParticular AffirmativeSome S are PUndistributedUndistributed
OParticular NegativeSome S are not PUndistributedDistributed

Distribution: A term is distributed when the proposition makes a claim about ALL members of that category. Badges show D for distributed and xD for undistributed.

📌 Note on Singular Propositions

In traditional categorical syllogisms, a singular proposition (e.g., "Socrates is a man") is generally treated as a universal proposition. This is because a singular term refers to exactly one individual, and the proposition makes a claim about the entire extension of that term.

📐 Syllogistic Figures

A syllogism's figure is determined by the position of the Middle Term (M) in the premises:

FigureMajor PremiseMinor PremisePattern
Figure 1M - PS - MMiddle is subject of major, predicate of minor
Figure 2P - MS - MMiddle is predicate of both
Figure 3M - PM - SMiddle is subject of both
Figure 4P - MM - SMiddle is predicate of major, subject of minor

🔗 Sorites (Chain Syllogisms)

A sorites (from Greek "heap") is an extended chain of syllogistic reasoning consisting of three or more premises that lead directly to a conclusion.

📖 Example of a Sorites

1. All A are B
2. All B are C
3. All C are D
4. All D are E
∴ All A are E

This sorites contains three hidden syllogisms:

  • Syllogism 1: All A are B, All B are C → All A are C
  • Syllogism 2: All A are C, All C are D → All A are D
  • Syllogism 3: All A are D, All D are E → All A are E

🛠️ Using the Platform for Sorites

In Statement Mode:

  • "Premise → Syllogism P1" - Copies the current statement to Premise 1
  • "Premise → Syllogism P2" - Copies the current statement to Premise 2

In Syllogism Mode:

  • "Conclusion → P1" - Copies the conclusion to Premise 1, allowing you to chain syllogisms

⚠️ Important: For a sorites to be valid, EVERY constituent syllogism must be valid.

🎯 How to Use the Platform

Step 1: Enter Propositions

  • In Statement Mode: Enter subject and predicate terms for a single premise
  • In Syllogism Mode: Enter terms for both premises (the system requires exactly 3 unique terms)
  • Select quantifiers (All/Some) and copulas (are/are not) from dropdowns
  • The conclusion terms are automatically populated from your premises

Step 2: Select Conclusion Terms

  • Choose the subject and predicate for your conclusion from the dropdown menus
  • The system will automatically identify Major, Minor, and Middle terms

Step 3: Check Validity (Syllogism Mode)

  • Click "Check Validity" to determine if your syllogism is valid
  • Results show the mood-figure code (e.g., AAA-1), traditional name (e.g., Barbara), and validity status
  • Green = Unconditionally Valid, Yellow = Conditionally Valid, Red = Invalid

Step 4: Use the Proving Table

  • Manually test each validity rule by selecting YES/NO in the Answer column
  • Or click "System Proving" to have the system automatically check all rules

🎨 Using the Diagram Canvas

The diagram canvas supports both mouse/trackpad and touch/finger interactions:

🖱️ Desktop (Mouse/Trackpad)

  • Click on regions to color them with the selected color
  • Drag circles from their center to reposition them
  • Drag circle edges to resize circles
  • Drag existence markers (X) to move them

📱 Mobile/Tablet (Touch/Finger)

  • Tap on regions to color them
  • Touch and drag circles from their center to reposition
  • Touch and drag circle edges to resize
  • Touch and drag existence markers (X) to move them
  • Swipe across regions to paint multiple areas in one motion

🎨 Color & Pattern Settings

  • Color palette: Select from 10 preset colors or use transparent/clear
  • Overlay mode: When checked, new colors are layered on top of existing colors
  • Transparency: Adjust from 0% (solid) to 100% (fully transparent)
  • Stripe pattern: Enable stripes and adjust angle from 0° to 359°
  • Use "Clear All" to reset diagram colors only
  • Use "Reset Diagram" to reset circle positions, sizes, colors, and existence markers

💡 Tip: Use Overlay mode with stripes to create cross-hatching patterns.

🤖 Auto-Diagramming

The Auto Draw feature automatically generates Venn diagram shadings and existence markers based on the propositions you have entered.

📍 Where to Find Auto Draw

The Auto Draw toolbar appears in the yellow-bordered bar on both Diagram 1 and Diagram 2. Each toolbar contains a Premises / Conclusion toggle that controls which set of buttons is active:

  • Premises toggle: Enables P1, P2, and Both buttons; disables the Conclusion button
  • Conclusion toggle: Enables the Conclusion button; disables P1, P2, and Both buttons

This means you can draw either premises or conclusion on any diagram, giving you full flexibility.

🔘 Auto Draw Buttons Explained

Premise Buttons (active when "Premises" toggle is selected):

  • P1 — Draws Premise 1 only, using 45° stripe shading for universal propositions
  • P2 — Draws Premise 2 only, using 135° stripe shading for universal propositions
  • P1 & P2 — Draws both premises together with cross-hatching in overlapping shaded areas

Conclusion Button (active when "Conclusion" toggle is selected):

  • Conclusion — Draws the conclusion proposition using 0° (horizontal) stripe shading

📊 What Auto Draw Produces

Proposition TypeDiagram ActionExample
A (All S are P)Shades the region(s) of S that are outside P"All dogs are animals" → shade the dogs-only region
E (No S are P)Shades the overlapping region of S and P"No cats are dogs" → shade the cats-and-dogs overlap
I (Some S are P)Places an X on the boundary of the S∩P region"Some students are athletes" → X on the overlap boundary
O (Some S are not P)Places an X on the boundary of the S-only region"Some birds are not flyers" → X on the birds-only boundary

🎓 Pedagogical Use: Comparing Premises vs. Conclusion

A recommended workflow for teaching or learning syllogism validity through diagrams:

  1. Enter all three propositions (two premises and a conclusion) in Syllogism Mode
  2. Go to Diagram 1, select the Premises toggle, and click "Both"
  3. Go to Diagram 2, select the Conclusion toggle (default), and click "Conclusion"
  4. Compare the two diagrams: if the conclusion's diagram is already entailed by the premises' diagram, the syllogism is valid

Alternatively, you can draw both premises and conclusion on the same diagram by switching the toggle to compare them directly.

⚠️ Note: Auto Draw works best with the standard Venn diagram layout (default circle positions and sizes). If you have moved or resized circles to create an Euler diagram, the auto-drawn shading will adapt to the current positions but may look different from a standard textbook Venn diagram.

💡 Tip: After auto-drawing, you can still manually adjust the diagram — add more shading, move existence markers, or overlay additional colours. Auto Draw gives you a starting point; manual editing lets you customize it further.

✅ Syllogism Validity Rules

A valid syllogism must satisfy ALL of the following six fundamental rules:

RuleRequirementFallacy if Violated
Three TermsExactly three distinct terms used consistentlyFallacy of Four Terms
Middle Term DistributionMiddle term must be distributed at least onceUndistributed Middle
Conclusion DistributionTerms distributed in conclusion must be distributed in premisesIllicit Major/Minor
Exclusive PremisesCannot have two negative premisesFallacy of Exclusive Premises
Negative MatchesNegative premise requires negative conclusion; affirmative premises require affirmative conclusionDrawing wrong quality conclusion
Existential RuleParticular conclusion from universal premises requires existential assumptionExistential Fallacy

⚡ Why Only 6 Rules? Understanding Derived Shortcuts

You may have encountered two additional "rules" in some textbooks. These are actually shortcuts derived from the 6 fundamental rules above.

Shortcut RuleWhat It SaysWhy It's Not Fundamental
Two Particulars RuleIf both premises start with "Some...", no valid conclusion can follow.Two "Some" statements never provide enough distribution. Will trigger Undistributed Middle or Illicit Process.
Particular Conclusion CorollaryIf one premise is Particular, your conclusion must also be Particular.Will be caught as Illicit Major or Illicit Minor.

💡 Key Insight: These shortcuts are useful for quickly spotting invalid arguments, but the 6 fundamental rules tell you precisely what went wrong.
